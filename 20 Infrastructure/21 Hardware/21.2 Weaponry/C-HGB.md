# Common Hypersonic Glide Body (C-HGB)

Standardized non-nuclear hypersonic glide vehicle developed jointly by the Army and Navy for conventional deep-strike capability. Developed by Lockheed Martin and Dynetics. The "common" design is shared across two platforms: the Army's Dark Eagle (Long-Range Hypersonic Weapon / LRHW) and the Navy's Conventional Prompt Strike (CPS) program.

## Technical Overview

The C-HGB detaches from a booster rocket at the edge of space, then glides through the upper atmosphere at Mach 5+. Unlike ballistic missiles which follow a predictable arc, the glide body performs high-G maneuvering during descent — making it extremely difficult for existing air defense systems to intercept. The weapon does not require a large explosive payload: kinetic energy at Mach 5+ is sufficient to collapse hardened underground bunkers.

**Range and speed:** 3,500+ km, Mach 5+. Launched from Guam, the weapon can strike mainland Chinese targets in under 20 minutes.

## Operational Status (2026)

**March 20, 2026:** U.S. Army officially declared the "hypersonic gap" closed with the first full Dark Eagle battery entering active service at Joint Base Lewis-McChord.

**USS Zumwalt:** The Navy's Zumwalt-class destroyers completed conversion in early 2026 — replacing the failed 155mm guns with large-diameter tubes specifically designed for C-HGB launch. The Zumwalt is the primary naval C-HGB platform.

**Unresolved testing concern:** A March 2026 DOT&E (Director, Operational Test and Evaluation) assessment noted "insufficient data" regarding C-HGB survivability in contested electromagnetic environments — meaning the weapon was fielded before testing was complete.
