# Common Hypersonic Glide Body (C-HGB)

## 🔍 Summary
The C-HGB is a standardized, non-nuclear warhead delivery vehicle designed to glide through the upper atmosphere at hypersonic speeds. By April 2026, it has become the "Joint Standard" for the U.S. military's conventional deep-strike capability, overcoming years of testing delays to provide a "Zero-Warning" tool for destroying high-value, hardened targets.

## 🧬 The "Lethality" Piston
* **The "Common" Design:** To reduce costs and speed up deployment, the Army and Navy share this single glide body design. It detaches from a booster rocket at the edge of space and performs high-G maneuvers during its descent, making it nearly impossible for modern air defenses to intercept.
* **Kinetic Energy Strike:** The C-HGB does not necessarily require a large explosive payload; at Mach 5+, the sheer kinetic energy of its impact is sufficient to collapse underground bunkers or "vaporize" mobile missile launchers.
* **Operational Reach:** When launched from Guam, a C-HGB-equipped missile can strike targets in mainland China in under **20 minutes**.

## 🔗 The "Technate" Loop
* **The "Hypersonic Gap" Closure:** On **March 20, 2026**, the U.S. Army officially declared the "Hypersonic Gap" closed as the first full battery of **[[Dark Eagle]]** missiles entered active service at Joint Base Lewis-McChord.
* **Naval Integration:** As of April 2026, the **USS Zumwalt** is completing its return from dry dock, where its failed 155mm guns were replaced by large-diameter tubes specifically for the C-HGB.
* **Lethality Friction:** A March 2026 DOT&E (Director of Operational Test and Evaluation) assessment noted that while the weapon is fielded, there is still "insufficient data" regarding its survivability in highly contested electromagnetic environments.
