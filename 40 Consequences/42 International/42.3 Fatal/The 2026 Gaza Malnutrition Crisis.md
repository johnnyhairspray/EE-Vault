# The 2026 Gaza Malnutrition Crisis

Ongoing humanitarian catastrophe resulting from the sustained 2023–2026 conflict and destruction of Gaza's civilian infrastructure. Despite the January 2025 ceasefire and subsequent Board of Peace governance framework, severe limitations on aid delivery have produced acute malnutrition across the population.

## Documented Scale (as of April 2026)

**Direct conflict deaths:** Cumulative toll exceeded 70,000 as of November 2025. Ongoing.

**Child malnutrition (UN screening, March 2026):** Of 35,110 children screened:
- 4% require acute malnutrition treatment
- 0.5% diagnosed with Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) — the most life-threatening category
- Projected: 100,000+ children facing acute malnutrition by April 2026

**Water access:** Drinking water availability in Gaza City fell below 6 liters per person per day in early 2026 — well below the minimum 15 liters per day recommended for survival. Cause: Mekorot pipeline damage and destruction of the Safa Well Field during military operations.

## The Infrastructure Destruction Context

The [[Where's Daddy.md]] / [[Gospel AI]] / [[Lavender AI]] AI targeting system documentation showed that The Gospel AI generated structural targeting at approximately 100 buildings per day. The UN Special Committee's "domicide" report (February 2026) documented systematic destruction of housing, water infrastructure, and civilian facilities.

The 2026 malnutrition crisis is the downstream consequence of that targeting pattern: a civilian population without functioning water infrastructure, agricultural capacity, or supply chain access.

## The Board of Peace Governance Problem

The [[Board of Peace]] established January 17, 2026 is the governing body responsible for humanitarian aid coordination and reconstruction. The malnutrition crisis represents the gap between the governance structure's announced authority and its operational capacity — and raises the question of whether aid delivery restrictions are a failure of implementation or a feature of the framework that keeps the population dependent on the Board's control over access.
