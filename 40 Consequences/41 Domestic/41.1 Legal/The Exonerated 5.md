# The Exonerated 5 (Central Park Jogger Case)

The wrongful conviction of five Black and Latino teenagers — Korey Wise, Yusef Salaam, Antron McCray, Raymond Santana, and Kevin Richardson — for the rape and assault of a jogger in Central Park on April 19, 1989. All five were coerced into confessions without lawyers present. DNA evidence excluded all five from the crime. They served between 6 and 13 years before being exonerated in 2002 when the actual perpetrator, Matias Reyes, confessed and his DNA matched.

## The Trump Connection

Donald Trump placed full-page ads in four New York City newspapers in May 1989 — before the trial — calling for the reinstatement of the death penalty and writing: "I want to hate these murderers and I always will." He paid approximately $85,000 for the ads.

After the exoneration in 2002, Trump continued to insist the five were guilty: "The fact that the case was settled with so much evidence against them is outrageous." He repeated this position through his 2016 campaign, 2019, and into the 2024 cycle.

[[Yusef Salaam]] — one of the five — was elected to the New York City Council in 2023. Trump continued attacking him by name during the 2024 campaign.

## The Vault Significance

The case is documented here not as historical context but because Trump's conduct — publicly calling for the death of minors based on racially charged assumptions, then refusing to acknowledge exoneration over 35+ years — is the documented behavioral pattern that predates and contextualizes his administration's approach to law enforcement, due process, and racial targeting.

The Central Park Five case is the clearest documented example of Trump publicly demanding state violence against individuals who turned out to be factually innocent, and refusing to revise that position when presented with conclusive evidence.
