# The Ruthless Review (JAG Purges)

Systematic overhaul of the U.S. military's Judge Advocate General (JAG) legal offices, ordered by Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth. Designed to eliminate independent military legal oversight and ensure military lawyers prioritize executive directives over legal constraints.

## The Friday Night Blitz (February 2025)

Hegseth simultaneously fired the top three uniformed military lawyers — the Judge Advocates General (TJAGs) of the Army, Navy, and Air Force — in a single evening. These are Senate-confirmed positions specifically designed to provide independent legal oversight of military operations.

Their function: advise commanders on the legality of operations, rules of engagement, and domestic deployments — providing an internal legal check on commanders who might otherwise exceed their authority.

Their removal eliminated the primary internal legal oversight layer from all three military branches simultaneously.

## The 2026 Expansion (March 11, 2026)

Hegseth directed a "no-excuses review" of all staff judge advocates down to brigade level — extending the purge from senior leadership to the entire operational legal tier. The mandate directs JAGs to focus on "procurement and fiscal law" while stripping their authority to review the legality of domestic deployments or rules of engagement.

**What this removes:** The ability of military lawyers to flag illegal orders — including orders to deploy troops domestically without invoking the Insurrection Act, or orders that violate the laws of armed conflict.

## The Accountability Structure Problem

JAG officers are:
- Commissioned military officers (can be ordered to follow commands)
- Licensed attorneys (subject to bar association ethics rules)
- Bound by the Uniform Code of Military Justice

The tension between "follow orders" and "refuse illegal orders" is where JAG authority lives. By removing TJAGs and purging the corps down to brigade level, Hegseth effectively replaced officers who might refuse illegal orders with officers who have been vetted for compliance.

Former JAG officers quoted by the ABA Journal: "Used, abused and sidelined."
