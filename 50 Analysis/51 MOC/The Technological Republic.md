# The Technological Republic: Karp vs. NRx vs. Technocracy

## Context

On April 18, 2026, Palantir posted a 22-point summary of CEO Alex Karp and Nicholas Zamiska's book *The Technological Republic: Hard Power, Soft Belief, and the Future of the West* (2025) to X, "because we get asked a lot." The reaction was immediate and global — labeled "technofascism" by Belgian philosopher Mark Coeckelbergh, described as "an AI death drive" by Novara Media, and called "more important than Trump" by Alexander Dugin.

Eliot Higgins (Bellingcat): *"Palantir sells operational software to defence, intelligence, immigration and police agencies. These 22 points aren't philosophy floating in space — they're the public ideology of a company whose revenue depends on the politics it's advocating."*

This MOC analyzes that ideology against the two frameworks it most resembles and most differs from: NRx (Curtis Yarvin's Dark Enlightenment) and Technocracy Inc. (Howard Scott's 1930s movement). The vault already has detailed nodes on both.

---

## The 22-Point Summary — Key Claims

1. Silicon Valley has an "affirmative obligation to participate in the defense of the nation"
2. Rebel against the tyranny of apps — the iPhone may be "limiting our sense of the possible"
3. "Free email is not enough" — tech owes the nation economic growth and security
4. If a Marine asks for a better rifle, build it — same goes for software
5. "The question is not whether AI weapons will be built; it is who will build them and for what purpose"
6. Hard power in this century will be built on software
7. National service should be a universal duty
8. The postwar neutering of Germany and Japan must be undone
9. One age of deterrence (nuclear) is ending; a new era built on AI is beginning
10. Silicon Valley elites "almost snicker at Musk's interest in grand narrative" — condemns this
11. The "psychologization of modern politics" must be resisted
12. The current ruling class produces "ineffectual, empty vessels"
13. Caution in public life is corrosive — those who say nothing wrong say nothing much at all
14. Elite intolerance of religious belief must be resisted
15. Some cultures have produced vital advances; others remain dysfunctional and regressive
16. "We must resist the shallow temptation of a vacant and hollow pluralism"
17. The West's half-century resistance to defining national cultures "in the name of inclusivity" is a failure
18. Inclusion into what?

Karp on Palantir's mission (separate earnings call): *"to scare enemies, and on occasion, kill them."* Palantir self-describes as providing "an AI-powered kill chain" enabling "decision dominance from space to mud."

## Where Karp Converges with NRx

**The Cathedral diagnosis:** Both reject the current meaning-making apparatus of liberal democracy as corrupt and insufficient. Yarvin calls it the Cathedral — universities and media producing false consensus. Karp calls it "hollow pluralism," the "psychologization of modern politics," the culture that snickers at grand narrative. Different vocabulary, identical critique.

**Anti-egalitarianism:** Both are explicitly hierarchical. Yarvin: hierarchy is natural; the sovereign CEO governs, citizens are shareholders. Karp: some cultures produce wonders, others are "dysfunctional and regressive." The egalitarian premise of liberal democracy is rejected by both.

**Tech elite as legitimate authority:** Both believe technical-class expertise confers special authority or obligation. The disagreement is in the direction of that obligation — Yarvin's elite governs, Karp's elite defends.

**Contempt for current political class:** Both view the existing political class as inadequate vessels. Yarvin's Cathedral produces captured bureaucrats; Karp's describes "ineffectual, empty vessels whose ambition one would forgive if there were any genuine belief structure lurking within."

---

## Where Karp Breaks from NRx — The Critical Divergence

**Exit vs. Defend:** This is the fundamental break. Yarvin's entire project is structured around exit — the patchwork, network states, Praxis, sovereign corporate entities that citizens leave rather than vote in. The nation-state is the problem; the solution is post-national privatized sovereignty.

Karp is an **aggressive nationalist** who wants tech to *serve* the American nation-state, not replace it. National service as universal duty. "Silicon Valley owes a moral debt to the country that made its rise possible." Hard power so *the West* prevails. Karp wants to arm the state Yarvin wants to dissolve.

This makes the Thiel/Karp relationship within Palantir ideologically paradoxical. Thiel is the Yarvin-adjacent exit theorist — he funds Praxis, backs network states, wrote "I no longer believe freedom and democracy are compatible." Karp is the nationalist who believes in arming democracy against its enemies. They built the same surveillance company from fundamentally different ideological positions.

**Nationalism vs. post-nationalism:** NRx is cosmopolitan in its architecture — the patchwork has no loyalty to any particular nation. Karp's framework is explicitly Western civilizationist, with America and the West as the unit that must prevail.

**Conscription:** Karp calls for universal national service. Yarvin would view this as exactly the kind of democratic-nationalist coercion his framework opposes. NRx favors voluntary association and exit; mandatory state service is antithetical to it.

## Where Karp Converges with Technocracy Inc.

**Technical expertise as legitimate authority:** Howard Scott believed engineers should govern because only engineers understand resource reality. Karp believes engineers have a moral obligation to govern — or at least to arm — because engineers are civilization's stewards.

**Civilizational rather than national scale:** Both operate at civilizational level. Karp's "the West"; Scott's Technate spanning the continent. See [[The Technate Redux]] for the territorial parallel.

**Inadequacy of current political structures:** Both view existing democratic politics as too timid, too process-captured, too unwilling to do what the technical moment requires. The current ruling class produces "ineffectual, empty vessels" (Karp); democracy produces captured bureaucrats incapable of technical reality (Scott).

---

## Where Karp Breaks from Technocracy Inc.

**Capitalism vs. anti-capitalism:** Technocracy Inc. was explicitly anti-capitalist — it wanted to abolish the price system entirely and replace it with energy accounting. Karp's entire operation is measured in market cap. Palantir is publicly traded and reports quarterly earnings. The financial infrastructure Scott wanted to dismantle is the one Karp inhabits and profits from.

**Military vs. pacifism:** Karp is explicitly militarist — AI deterrence, national service, "kill chains," "decision dominance." Howard Scott's Technate would be so resource-self-sufficient it wouldn't need wars. Their relationship to violence is opposite.

**Replace vs. Defend:** Scott wanted to transform democratic structures into technical administration. Karp wants to arm existing structures so they can win.

## What Karp Actually Is: The Schmitt Reading

The closest intellectual analog to Karp is not Yarvin and not Scott. It is **Carl Schmitt** — the Weimar jurist whose "friend/enemy distinction" holds that political identity is constituted through adversarial relationship. For Schmitt, the political does not exist without the enemy; sovereignty is defined by the capacity to designate and eliminate the threat.

Karp's West is defined primarily by what it defends against: China, adversaries who "will not pause to indulge in theatrical debates about the merits of developing technologies with critical military and national security applications." The identity is constituted by the threat. The enemy makes the nation real.

This is Schmittian, not Yarvinite or Technocratic. It is also structurally congruent with fascist state theory — not in the sense of historical fascism's specific features, but in the sense that the Schmittian logic (the political is defined by the enemy; sovereignty requires decisive violence against the threat) is the intellectual architecture of what the manifesto describes.

"Technofascism" is not a precise term, but it is pointing at something real: the combination of:
- Technical-class authority claims (from Technocracy)
- Anti-democratic cultural hierarchy (from NRx)
- Nationalist militarism with conscription demand (from historical fascism / Schmitt)
- Capital accumulation as the operating mechanism (from neither Technocracy nor NRx)

...produces something that doesn't have a clean prior label.

## The Bellingcat Point: Not Philosophy, Sales Pitch

Higgins' critique is the most analytically important: these 22 points are not philosophy floating in space. They are the public ideology of a company whose revenue depends on the politics being advocated.

Mapping the ideology to the revenue:

| Ideological Claim | Revenue Mechanism |
|---|---|
| AI weapons are inevitable; question is who builds them | Palantir as preferred builder |
| Hard power requires software | Palantir as software |
| National service as universal duty | Larger military → more Palantir contracts |
| AI deterrence replacing nuclear | Palantir as deterrence infrastructure |
| West must prevail | US/NATO governments buy Palantir |
| Some cultures are regressive | Targeting populations defined as regressive |
| Reject hollow pluralism | Fewer constraints on surveillance deployment |

The manifesto is simultaneously Karp's genuine worldview AND a perfect sales pitch for why governments should spend more on Palantir. Whether that's cynicism or sincerity is the wrong question. The two are structurally indistinguishable from where Karp sits.

TechPolicy.Press: *"The message of both the book and the manifesto is that Palantir wants to be THE weapons manufacturer of the next century. The future of the weapons business is software plus AI. Palantir would like the government to spend an exceptional amount of money on Palantir products."*
