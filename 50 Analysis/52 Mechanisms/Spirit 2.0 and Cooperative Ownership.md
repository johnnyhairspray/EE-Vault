# Spirit 2.0 — Community Ownership as Counter-Extraction

## What Happened

Spirit Airlines suspended all operations at 3:00 AM on May 2, 2026 — its second bankruptcy. 17,000 jobs lost. 44 million annual passengers without a carrier. Creditors rejected an 11th-hour rescue deal.

Within hours, **Hunter Peterson** — a TikToker/YouTuber who previously went viral for flying Spirit for 24 hours straight — launched **Spirit 2.0** at letsbuyspiritair.com after his TikTok video proposing the idea went viral.

**Correction from initial vault note:** The founder is not an autistic pilot — he is a content creator. The pilot detail was incorrect.

---

## The Numbers

- **$22 million** in pledges from 36,605 founding patrons — before the site first crashed
- **$88 million** in pledges — before the site crashed again on May 4
- Site crashed twice due to overwhelming traffic
- Minimum pledge: $45 (average price of a one-way Spirit ticket — the entry point is the product)
- Trending on Google at 1,000%+ increase in searches

---

## The Model

- **Entry point:** $45 per share pledge
- **Governance:** One member, one vote — explicitly anti-private-equity
- **Profit distribution:** Shared by all members
- **Structure modeled on:** Green Bay Packers, WinCo Foods

The $45 entry point is the key innovation. It converts the question from "are you an investor?" to "have you flown Spirit?" Everyone who's bought a ticket already understands the price. They're not investing — they're buying back in at a scale they recognize.

---

## The Class Solidarity Problem

The observation: this is a "stupid thing to converge on" but it's class solidarity in action.

It's not stupid — it's accessible. Most people cannot rally around abstract economic reform. But "we should own the airline that flew us to see our families at a price we could afford" is concrete, immediate, and personal. The convergence point is low-stakes enough to be safe and high-profile enough to be meaningful.

This is how class solidarity actually forms in practice — not around ideology, but around a shared material interest that everyone can immediately understand and act on. The $45 entry point made it accessible to the exact demographic Spirit served: budget travelers who couldn't afford the incumbents.

The real test is not the pledges. It's whether the legal and financial infrastructure can convert $88M in momentum into an actual bankruptcy court bid before PE capital locks up the assets. Pledges are proof of demand. Acquisition is a different problem.

---

## The Crypto Attachment Problem

A SPIRIT memecoin on Solana launched simultaneously — anonymous founders, no governance rights, no legal standing in any acquisition. The extraction engine attaching itself to a genuine grassroots movement before it can solidify. The memecoin is not Spirit 2.0. The distinction matters.

---

## What This Proves

Regardless of outcome, Spirit 2.0 has demonstrated:
1. $88M in community capital can be mobilized in 48 hours around a cooperative ownership model
2. The $45 accessible entry point generates mass participation
3. The one-member-one-vote governance model is legible and appealing to non-investors
4. The window between asset distress and PE lock-up can be contested if the movement is ready

The revolution will be bloodless as long as the elites allow it to be.
