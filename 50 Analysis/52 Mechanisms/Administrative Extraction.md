# Administrative Extraction

## Definition

Administrative extraction is the transfer of logistical, cognitive, and labor burdens from institutions onto individuals. It is one of the least-discussed but most powerful gears in the [[The Extraction Engine]].

Tasks once handled by professionals or institutions — billing, claims, troubleshooting, scheduling, matching, verification, configuration, optimization — are now outsourced to users as unpaid labor. The shift is so normalized it is rarely perceived as extraction at all.

---

## The Mechanism

### What Gets Transferred

Contemporary examples of institutional labor transferred to individuals:

- Navigating insurance formularies (task once performed by healthcare administrators)
- Managing job applications through opaque ATS algorithms (task once performed by recruiters)
- Moderating one's own online social spaces (task once performed by editors and community managers)
- Performing customer support for malfunctioning technology (task once performed by company staff)
- Tracking subscriptions (task once performed by accounting)
- Choosing among complex utility rate plans (task once requiring a regulated tariff system)
- Serving as unpaid beta-tester for software released before completion
- Curating personal digital personas (task once performed by PR professionals, when it existed at all)
- Self-diagnosing health conditions before seeking care (task once performed in triage)
- Tax preparation for increasingly complex personal financial situations (task once requiring professional assistance)

### The Cognitive Consequence

Administrative extraction produces:
- Chronic overload — constant low-grade cognitive demand
- Decision fatigue — after hundreds of micro-decisions, willpower and judgment degrade
- Chronic uncertainty — systems are designed to be difficult to understand
- Reduced resistance capacity — exhausted people cannot organize, advocate, or fight back
- Diminished time for community, rest, or dissent

Research on administrative burden as policy instrument: Herd & Moynihan (2018) document that administrative burden functions as a mechanism that disproportionately harms marginalized groups and often serves political or economic ends — not merely bureaucratic inefficiency.

### The Economic Logic: Burden Creates Demand

Administrative extraction doesn't just shift cost — it **manufactures new markets**. People overwhelmed by self-management then purchase:

- Executive-function apps
- Task management tools
- Coaching services
- Convenience subscriptions and delivery services
- Automation tools
- Telehealth micro-interventions
- Productivity tracking software

**The burden creates the demand. The demand generates revenue.** The institution that created the burden then often sells the solution to the burden it created.

This is the extraction engine's most elegant move: the same corporate actor who transferred the labor also sells you the tools to manage the labor they transferred.

## Empirical Evidence

**Healthcare:** Prior authorizations, refill schedule monitoring, pill counts, pain contracts, tapering protocols. Patients become administrators of their own suffering. The administrative burden of US healthcare is not incidental — administrative costs consume approximately 34% of total US healthcare spending, compared to 12% in Canada.

**Banking:** Overdraft monitoring, password management, fraud dispute processes, account opening bureaucracy. The CFPB documented that the lowest-income households pay approximately 10 times more in banking fees relative to account balances than higher-income households.

**Employment:** ATS (Applicant Tracking System) gatekeeping means applicants perform the labor of recruiters — formatting resumes to algorithmic specifications, completing long-form applications for positions that may not exist, managing rejection without human feedback.

**Housing:** Tenant screening processes, application fees, credit documentation, income verification — all transferred to the applicant, who pays (in time and money) for processes that benefit only the landlord.

**Platforms:** Rating systems, content moderation reports, privacy settings, cookie consent dialogs, terms-of-service agreements. The average ToS document requires a law degree to parse. The labor of understanding what one has agreed to is transferred entirely to the user.

## The Political Dimension

Administrative extraction has a direct political expression in the vault's investigative work:

**[[Project 2025]] and [[Schedule F]]** are administrative extraction applied to democratic governance. Dismantling the administrative state — firing civil servants, reclassifying positions to enable political replacement, eliminating regulatory expertise — transfers the burden of navigating public services from functioning institutions onto individuals and localities. When the EPA's enforcement capacity is gutted, communities must individually navigate contamination without federal support. When the CFPB is weakened, consumers must individually manage predatory lending without regulatory protection.

The design is the same: institutional capacity is removed, the burden falls to individuals, and those with the fewest resources bear the highest cost.

**[[The Privatized Panopticon]]** inverts the mechanism: surveillance infrastructure removes the burden of accountability from power while increasing the administrative burden on citizens navigating systems that track them. You must manage your digital footprint; the institution accumulates data without management cost.

## The Poverty Amplification

Administrative extraction is not experienced equally. See [[The Poverty Premium]] for full analysis.

For those with limited resources:
- **Missing a bill deadline** due to administrative burden means a late fee, a credit hit, or service disconnection — not an inconvenience but a crisis
- **Failing to navigate a healthcare prior authorization** means foregoing treatment, not delaying it
- **Not completing a benefits application correctly** means losing housing assistance, not a bureaucratic hassle

The same administrative burden that is merely annoying for a household with slack is catastrophic for a household without it. Administrative extraction therefore functions as a **regressive tax** — heavier on those least able to pay it.

## Why It's the Least Discussed Mechanism

Of the four reinforcing mechanisms in the extraction engine (illusory choice, instant gratification, planned obsolescence, administrative extraction), this one generates the least cultural or political attention — for structural reasons:

1. **It's invisible by design.** The burden is framed as personal responsibility, not institutional transfer.
2. **It's diffuse.** No single actor imposed it; it emerged from competitive pressure across sectors.
3. **It's normalized.** Each generation accepts a higher baseline of self-management than the previous one.
4. **Complaining about it sounds like laziness.** The ideology of self-optimization frames administrative burden as something to be managed better, not resisted structurally.
5. **Its products are marketed as solutions.** The app that manages your subscriptions, the service that files your taxes, the platform that curates your self-brand — all are sold as liberation from the burden they help perpetuate.
