# Cooperative Ownership as Counter-Extraction

## The Thesis

The Extraction Engine operates by concentrating ownership of productive assets in private capital while extracting labor, attention, and resources from the people who use and work within those assets. Cooperative ownership inverts this structure: the workers and users own the asset, make governance decisions democratically, and share the surplus.

This is not a utopian proposal. It is a proven model operating at scale globally.

---

## Documented Examples at Scale

| Organization | Type | Scale |
|---|---|---|
| Mondragon Corporation | Worker cooperative conglomerate | $12B annual revenue, 80,000+ workers |
| REI | Consumer cooperative | $4B+ annual revenue, 22 million members |
| Credit unions | Member-owned financial institutions | $2.2 trillion in U.S. assets, 135 million members |
| Green Bay Packers | Community-owned sports franchise | Only non-profit, publicly owned franchise in NFL |
| Ocean Spray | Farmer cooperative | $2B+ annual revenue |
| Ace Hardware | Retailer cooperative | $9B+ annual revenue |
| Land trusts | Community land ownership | Demonstrated housing stability model |
| Co-op Grocery Networks | Consumer cooperative | Nationwide, varies by region |

Cooperative ownership is not rare. It is structurally suppressed.

---

## Why It Doesn't Happen More — The Structural Barriers

The barriers to cooperative ownership are not failures of the model. They are features of the system designed to maintain private capital's preferential access to productive assets.

**Speed asymmetry:** When a distressed asset hits the market, private equity firms have capital staged and legal structures ready to deploy in days. Community movements must raise awareness, build trust, organize pledges, establish legal entities, and do it all before the asset is locked up. The window is deliberately short.

**Legal infrastructure bias:** Corporate law, bankruptcy law, and securities regulation were built for private capital acquisition. Cooperative formation faces higher regulatory friction, fewer standardized legal templates, and less institutional support than private equity buyouts.

**Capital access:** Credit markets preferentially serve private equity structures. Cooperative movements must raise capital from members — often in small amounts — against professional investors with staged fund structures.

**Information asymmetry:** Distressed asset buyers have access to due diligence processes and financial information that community groups typically cannot access on the same timeline.

**The manufactured urgency:** The "fire sale" framing of distressed asset sales creates artificial time pressure that advantages prepared capital over organizing communities. This urgency is a feature of the system, not a neutral market condition.

---

## The Design Principles That Make Cooperatives Work

From successful models, the key structural elements:

**Low entry point:** $45 at Spirit 2.0. $20 at REI. Membership must be accessible to the people who use the asset — not just to accredited investors. The entry price sets the class composition of the membership.

**One member, one vote:** Governance rights must be decoupled from financial contribution. Otherwise large contributors recreate the private equity dynamic inside a cooperative shell — purchasing control through scale of investment.

**Profit distribution by use or labor:** Surplus returns to the people who generated it — workers by labor contribution, consumers by purchase volume — rather than to passive capital holders.

**Democratic accountability:** Governance decisions are made by the membership, not by a board accountable to shareholders.

## The Crypto Attachment Problem

Every genuine cooperative or community ownership movement generates an accompanying crypto token that captures the attention and sometimes the funds of people who want to participate — while providing none of the governance rights, legal standing, or structural power of actual cooperative ownership.

The token is the extraction engine's response to cooperative movements: provide an illusion of participation (you hold SPIRIT tokens, you're part of the movement) without any of the structural transformation cooperative ownership actually creates.

Distinguishing legitimate cooperative organizing from attached speculative instruments is a prerequisite for participating in counter-extraction movements effectively.

---

## The Counter-Extraction Potential

Cooperative ownership is the most direct structural counter to the Extraction Engine because it addresses the root mechanism: concentrated private ownership of productive assets that extract from users and workers.

Where the extraction engine says "you rent, we own, we extract," cooperative ownership says "you own, you govern, you share the surplus."

The Spirit 2.0 moment is significant not because it will necessarily succeed in acquiring Spirit Airlines, but because it demonstrated that the cooperative model can be organized and launched within hours of an asset becoming available — and that the $45 accessible entry point can mobilize mass participation faster than private equity expects.

The window between asset distress and private capital lock-up is the operative terrain. Organized communities with pre-built legal structures, accessible entry points, and clear governance models can compete in that window. They rarely have these things ready. Spirit 2.0 is the proof of concept that they can.
