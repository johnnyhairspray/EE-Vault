# The Fellowship / The Family

## Summary

The Fellowship (also known as The Family) is a secretive, elite Christian fundamentalist organization that has operated at the highest levels of American political power since 1935. Unlike the [[Seven Mountains Mandate]]'s mass mobilization strategy, The Fellowship operates through intimate, private relationships with powerful individuals — senators, heads of state, generals, and CEOs — cultivated over decades with near-zero public profile.

Its theology is not evangelical in the conventional sense. It is explicitly **elitist**: Jesus chose the powerful as His instruments, not the meek. The Fellowship's function is to provide a private theological framework and loyalty network for the ruling class — across party lines.

Jeff Sharlet's *The Family: The Secret Fundamentalism at the Heart of American Power* (2008) and *C Street: The Fundamentalist Threat to American Democracy* (2010) are the primary documented sources.

---

## Origins

**1935:** Norwegian immigrant Abraham Vereide, a minister in Seattle, has a vision during a general strike: God tells him that the Church has been working the wrong end of the social ladder. Instead of organizing the poor, God wants Vereide to organize the **powerful** — mayors, businessmen, industrialists. The poor will follow whoever leads the elite.

Vereide begins hosting breakfast prayer meetings for Seattle's business and political elite. Within years he has relationships with congressmen and senators in Washington D.C.

**1942:** Vereide moves to Washington and establishes a formal prayer breakfast network. By the late 1940s he has penetrated the State Department, the military, and Congress.

**1953:** The first **National Prayer Breakfast** is held — still held annually, still organized by The Fellowship, still attended by every sitting president since Eisenhower. It is the most visible public face of a profoundly secretive organization.

**1969:** Doug Coe takes over leadership from Vereide and runs The Fellowship for decades. Under Coe, the organization becomes more secretive, more globally connected, and explicitly more focused on cultivating personal relationships with heads of state worldwide — including authoritarian ones.

## Theology

The Fellowship's theology is a radical inversion of conventional Christianity. Key doctrines as documented by Sharlet from internal documents and member accounts:

**Elite election:** Jesus does not choose the humble. He chooses the strong. Hitler, Stalin, Mao, and Pol Pot are invoked — not as villains but as examples of men who understood the power of total personal commitment to a cause. The Fellowship asks: what if that commitment were directed toward Jesus?

**The "Jesus plus nothing" doctrine:** Strip away all doctrine, denomination, and theology. What remains is pure personal relationship with Jesus — and personal relationship with other powerful men who share that relationship. No creed. No accountability to scripture. No church authority. Just the brotherhood.

**Confidentiality as discipline:** Members do not discuss The Fellowship publicly. There is no membership list, no published theology, no external accountability. The secrecy is framed as spiritual discipline — the way the early church operated in secret.

**Power as divine signal:** If a man holds power, that power is presumptively God-given. The Fellowship does not ask whether a leader is ethical. It asks whether the leader is open to relationship. Once in relationship, the leader can be shaped from within. This is the theological justification for cultivating dictators, war criminals, and corrupt politicians.

## C Street House

The most concrete manifestation of The Fellowship's DC operations. A townhouse at 133 C Street SE on Capitol Hill — one block from the Capitol — operating as a subsidized residence for members of Congress. Officially incorporated as a church, allowing it to pay no property taxes and face minimal disclosure requirements.

**Documented residents have included:**
- Senator John Ensign (R-NV) — resided at C Street during his extramarital affair with a staffer's wife; Fellowship members attempted to manage the cover-up
- Senator Tom Coburn (R-OK)
- Representative Bart Stupak (D-MI)
- Representative Zach Wamp (R-TN)
- Representative Mike Doyle (D-PA)
- Representative Heath Shuler (D-NC)

The house functions as spiritual accountability community, political networking hub, and crisis management resource — simultaneously. When Ensign's affair became public in 2009, Fellowship leaders including Doug Coe's son Tim Coe were involved in negotiating payments to the staffer's family. The arrangement blurred the line between pastoral care and political damage control with federal ethics implications.

**Mike Pence** has been associated with Fellowship prayer groups and has spoken at Fellowship-connected events, though he was not a C Street resident.

**Hillary Clinton** described a long-term personal relationship with Doug Coe and Fellowship prayer groups in her 2003 memoir *Living History*, calling Coe "a unique presence in Washington" and describing her participation in a women's prayer group connected to the Fellowship.

## Global Operations

The Fellowship's most underreported dimension is its international reach. Sharlet documented that Fellowship-connected relationships extend to:

- **African heads of state** — including figures connected to Uganda's Anti-Homosexuality Act (2009, reintroduced 2023). Fellowship members including Senator Jim Inhofe maintained close relationships with Ugandan officials. Sharlet documented direct Fellowship theological influence on the bill's sponsors — the idea that homosexuality represents satanic influence that the state has a divine mandate to eliminate.

- **Latin American military governments** — Vereide and early Fellowship figures cultivated relationships with right-wing military governments during the Cold War as part of an explicitly anti-communist strategy.

- **Former Soviet states** — Fellowship-connected figures established prayer breakfast networks in post-Soviet Eastern Europe and Central Asia, cultivating relationships with authoritarian leaders.

The pattern: The Fellowship does not publicly advocate policy. It cultivates private relationships with power, provides theological framing that supports hierarchy and executive authority, and allows those relationships to shape policy organically — with no traceable organizational fingerprint.

## The National Prayer Breakfast

Held annually in Washington D.C. since 1953. Every sitting president since Eisenhower has attended. Thousands of political and business leaders, domestic and international. Organized entirely by The Fellowship — a fact that was largely unknown to the public until Sharlet's reporting.

The Prayer Breakfast is The Fellowship's public legitimacy anchor. It creates the appearance of nonpartisan, civic Christianity while providing annual access to the highest levels of American power for an organization with no public accountability, no published membership, and no disclosed theology.

In 2023, Congress voted to sever the official congressional sponsorship of the National Prayer Breakfast, partially in response to years of scrutiny following Sharlet's reporting. The Fellowship continues to host it independently.

## Why It Matters

The Fellowship is the most significant religious-political organization in American history that most Americans cannot name. It has:

- Organized the annual gathering that every president attends since 1953
- Housed members of Congress in a subsidized Capitol Hill residence with no public disclosure
- Cultivated personal relationships with authoritarian leaders on every continent
- Provided theological framing for executive authority that predates and parallels the NRx intellectual project
- Operated for 90 years with near-zero public accountability

It is not a conspiracy. The meetings are real, the relationships are real, the theology is documented. It is an elite private network that uses religious identity as its organizing principle and confidentiality as its operating norm — exactly the structure that enables long-term coordination without formal institutional footprint.
