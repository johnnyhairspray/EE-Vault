# Mont Pelerin Society

Founded April 10, 1947, at the Hotel du Parc in the Swiss village of Mont Pèlerin, near Vevey, Switzerland. Organized by Friedrich Hayek. Thirty-nine founding participants including Milton Friedman, Ludwig von Mises, Karl Popper, George Stigler, Frank Knight, Lionel Robbins, and Aaron Director. Funded by the William Volker Fund, the Bank of England, the Foundation for Economic Education, and Credit Suisse — which paid 93% of the conference costs.

The Mont Pelerin Society is the **intellectual origin point** of the policy infrastructure that produced everything documented in this vault from [[The Heritage Foundation]] and [[Project 2025]] to [[DonorsTrust]] and the Atlas Network. It is not a top-down conspiracy. It is something more durable and more dangerous: a **thought collective** — a self-sustaining network of ideas, institutions, and people that reproduced itself across 75 years into the dominant operating system of contemporary conservative governance.

Philip Mirowski and Dieter Plehwe (Harvard University Press, 2009): *"The Road from Mont Pelerin"* — a "directory of organized neoliberalism."

## The Context of Founding

1947. The Bretton Woods international monetary order had just been established. Keynesian economics dominated Western governments. The New Deal coalition was entrenched in Washington. The Soviet model was ascendant in Eastern Europe. Hayek had just published *The Road to Serfdom* (1944), arguing that central planning inevitably led to totalitarianism — a text Churchill's Conservatives used in the 1945 British election and which made Hayek internationally prominent.

The founding participants were united by the belief that individual freedom in the developed world was under existential threat from collectivist trends — socialism, central planning, and what they saw as the creeping statism of the welfare state. They were not united on much else. Ludwig von Mises famously stood up at one session, announced *"You're all a bunch of socialists,"* and walked out.

The Society explicitly stated it had *"no official views, formulates no policies, publishes no manifestos, aligns itself with no party, and accepts no political or public funding."* It described itself as engaging in the *"battle of ideas."*

This was strategically accurate as far as it went. The MPS was not a lobbying organization. It was an **idea incubator and network hub** — the place where the people who would build the institutions that would do the lobbying first met and coordinated their worldview.

## The Deployment Strategy

Hayek's insight — confirmed by the MPS experience — was that **ideas precede policy by decades**. You could not win in politics directly. You had to first change what was thinkable, then change what was politically feasible, then change policy. This required:

1. **Academic legitimacy** — producing Nobel laureates. MPS helped create the Nobel Prize in Economics specifically to legitimize free-market thinking. Eight MPS members won it: Hayek, Friedman, Stigler, Allais, Buchanan, Coase, Becker, Smith.

2. **Think tank infrastructure** — translating academic ideas into policy language accessible to politicians and the press. Hayek encouraged MPS member **Antony Fisher** to pursue the think tank route rather than entering politics directly. Fisher went on to found:
   - **Institute of Economic Affairs (IEA)** — London, 1955. Provided the intellectual infrastructure for Margaret Thatcher's 1979 victory. Thatcher credited IEA directly for her election.
   - **Manhattan Institute** — New York, 1977
   - **Atlas Economic Research Foundation** — 1981, now the **Atlas Network**: 589 think tanks in 103 countries as of 2023

3. **Personnel pipeline** — training a generation of economists, lawyers, and policy advisers who would move through academia, think tanks, and government across multiple administrations

4. **Media penetration** — MPS founding members included Pulitzer Prize-winning journalists and editors at The New York Times, Washington Post, Life, Fortune, and Newsweek

## The Genealogy: MPS → Atlas → Heritage → Project 2025

The institutional lineage is documented and traceable:

**MPS → IEA → Atlas Network → Heritage Foundation → Project 2025**

- **Antony Fisher** (MPS member) founded IEA (1955), Manhattan Institute (1977), Atlas Foundation (1981)
- **Edwin Feulner** (MPS member since 1972) co-founded **[[The Heritage Foundation]]** in 1973 with Paul Weyrich — inspired directly by the Powell Memo. Feulner served as MPS president 1996-1998 and is current MPS treasurer. When Trump won in 2016, Feulner was the first person on his transition team
- **Charles Koch** (long-standing MPS member) co-founded the Cato Institute, funds Mercatus Center at George Mason University, and has poured **$100 million+ into at least 54 groups connected to individual MPS members**
- **[[DonorsTrust]]** — Atlas Network member organizations receive funding from DonorsTrust, creating the financial pipeline: MPS → Atlas Network → DonorsTrust → Project 2025 advisory organizations
- **Project 2025** — directly descended from Heritage Foundation, itself descended from the Fisher/Feulner/MPS lineage

The Atlas Network as of 2023: **589 organizations, 103 countries, $28 million annual budget**. It operates as the global franchise system for MPS-derived neoliberal policy.

## The Powell Memo (1971): Weaponization

In 1971, Virginia attorney Lewis Powell wrote a confidential memo for the U.S. Chamber of Commerce titled *"Attack on the American Free Enterprise System."* It was widely circulated among business leaders and became the blueprint for the systematic right-wing takeover of American political infrastructure — funding think tanks, law schools, media, and political candidates to shift the ideological terrain.

Powell was appointed to the Supreme Court by Nixon two months after writing it.

The Powell Memo was not an MPS document. But its strategy was the Hayek strategy operationalized with corporate money: **change what is thinkable before changing what is political.** The think tanks funded by Koch and Coors in response to the Powell Memo — Heritage (1973), Cato (1977), Heartland, State Policy Network — are the institutional implementation of the MPS idea incubator model with fossil fuel and industrial capital behind it.

## The NRx Divergence

An important analytical distinction: the vault's NRx network — Yarvin, Land, Thiel — is **not** a straightforward continuation of the MPS tradition. It represents a **fracture** within the right-wing intellectual ecosystem.

MPS was founded on Hayekian liberalism — a defense of individual freedom, rule of law, and market competition against state collectivism. Yarvin's neocameralism explicitly rejects democracy and liberalism. Thiel's *"freedom and democracy are incompatible"* is the inverse of Hayek's project.

What they share: the destination of **reduced democratic accountability** and **capital-based governance**. What they disagree on: whether this is achieved through market liberalism (MPS) or outright monarchism (NRx).

The current Trump coalition sits at the intersection of both traditions — using MPS-derived think tank infrastructure (Heritage, Project 2025) while absorbing NRx governance theory (Schedule F, DOGE, Yarvin's RAGE) into its operational framework. The coalition is not coherent. It is converging.

## The Pinochet Test Case

The clearest historical demonstration of what MPS ideas look like when deployed without democratic constraint: Chile under Pinochet (1973-1990). Friedman's "Chicago Boys" — trained at the University of Chicago, steeped in MPS economics — implemented shock therapy privatization while Pinochet's regime murdered, tortured, and disappeared thousands of leftists. Hayek visited Pinochet's Chile and praised it. Friedman visited and praised it. Thatcher befriended Pinochet.

The MPS intellectual tradition has always been comfortable with the answer to the question: what happens when free market economics requires violence to implement? The answer documented in Chile is: the violence is acceptable.

This is not incidental. It is instructive. The same intellectual tradition that produced Heritage, Cato, and Project 2025 also produced the Chicago Boys. The current deployment of that tradition through DOGE, Schedule F, and the deportation infrastructure is generation five of the same project.
