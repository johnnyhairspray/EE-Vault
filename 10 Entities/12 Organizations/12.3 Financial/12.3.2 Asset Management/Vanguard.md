# Vanguard

Founded 1975 by John C. Bogle in Malvern, Pennsylvania. World's second-largest asset manager. Approximately **$12 trillion in assets under management** as of 2025. The most structurally unusual of the Big Three: Vanguard is **owned by its own funds**, which are owned by its investors — making it effectively a mutual company without external shareholders. Bogle created this structure explicitly to eliminate the profit motive that he believed caused other fund companies to charge excessive fees.

## The Bogle Innovation

Bogle's insight: if a fund company is owned by outside investors, those investors extract profit from fund shareholders through fees. If the fund company is owned by the fund shareholders themselves, fees trend toward cost. This is why Vanguard has the lowest average expense ratios in the industry — there is no external owner to pay dividends to.

The irony: Bogle's innovation to democratize investing created the second-largest concentration of capital ownership in human history. By making index investing affordable and structurally sound, Vanguard attracted tens of trillions in assets from ordinary retirement savers — and with those assets came the voting rights over corporate America that now concern regulators across the political spectrum.

Bogle himself, before his death in 2019, expressed concern about the concentration his creation had produced: *"I do not believe that such concentration would be good for the nation."*

## The Dark Money Connection

The [[DonorsTrust]] note documents a finding by DeSmog that Fidelity, Schwab, and **Vanguard** DAFs collectively gave **$171 million to Project 2025 advisory groups** — more than double DonorsTrust's own contributions to the same groups. Vanguard's charitable arm is a donor-advised fund that, like all DAFs, does not disclose individual donors.

This does not make Vanguard a right-wing organization. It makes Vanguard a financial vehicle whose DAF infrastructure is used by wealthy donors across the political spectrum — including donors who funded Project 2025 — without Vanguard's knowledge of or consent to the specific grants. This is the same dynamic across all major financial DAFs.

The vault significance: the financial infrastructure that ordinary Americans use to save for retirement — Vanguard index funds — is also the infrastructure through which wealthy donors route dark money into political causes, including Project 2025. The same vehicle. Different functions. The ordinary investor has no visibility into the DAF grants being made through the same institutional umbrella.

## The Ownership Question

Who owns Vanguard? Its funds. Who owns its funds? Its investors. This circular structure means:
- Vanguard has no external shareholders to pay dividends to
- Vanguard has no parent company with its own political or financial interests
- Vanguard is not publicly traded
- Vanguard's financial disclosures are therefore less transparent than BlackRock's or State Street's

The FDIC has been pressuring Vanguard (alongside BlackRock) to sign agreements providing greater regulatory insight into its banking-related holdings — specifically because it holds large stakes in bank stocks with voting power. Vanguard's mutual ownership structure complicates standard regulatory approaches.

## The Common Ownership Problem

Vanguard owns large stakes in every major competitor in every sector it invests in simultaneously. It holds approximately 8-9% of Microsoft, Apple, Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta — all at once. It holds large stakes in Delta, United, Southwest, and American Airlines simultaneously. In banking, oil, pharmaceuticals, defense.

Academic research on **"common ownership"** — the phenomenon of a single investor owning competing firms — finds that common ownership reduces competitive pressure. Companies know their largest shareholders also own their competitors. The incentive to compete aggressively against a competitor when your biggest shareholder owns both of you is reduced. Prices tend to be higher in industries with high common ownership concentration.

This is not a conspiracy. It is an emergent structural property of index investing at scale. No one at Vanguard is calling airline CEOs and telling them not to compete on price. The incentive structure does the work without anyone making a call.

## Vault Relevance

Vanguard is the purest structural expression of the concentration problem the vault documents in capital terms: a mutual organization designed to serve ordinary investors that has, by succeeding at that mission, accumulated structural influence over corporate America that no regulatory framework anticipated or accounts for. It is the Mirror Hypothesis in financial form — a structure built to democratize capital ownership that has instead concentrated governance power.
