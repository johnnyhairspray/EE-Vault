# State Street

Founded 1792 — the oldest of the Big Three by nearly two centuries. Headquartered Boston. Third-largest asset manager globally. **$5.7 trillion in assets under management** as of year-end 2025 (a record for the firm). Operates under its asset management subsidiary **State Street Global Advisors (SSGA)**.

State Street's vault relevance is the same structural phenomenon as BlackRock and Vanguard — concentrated passive ownership of corporate America — but with a distinct origin and a specifically significant invention.

## The SPY

On January 22, 1993, State Street launched the **SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust (SPY)** — the **first U.S. exchange-traded fund**. This was not an incremental product improvement. It was the invention of a financial instrument that has since become a $13 trillion industry and the primary mechanism through which the Big Three concentration was built.

SPY remains the **most heavily traded ETF in the world** with daily volume often exceeding $28 billion. However, its legacy structure (organized as a unit investment trust in 1993) prevents it from reinvesting dividends, lending securities, or using sampling. Vanguard's VOO and BlackRock's IVV, organized more efficiently, have surpassed SPY in total assets despite SPY's trading volume dominance.

The irony: State Street invented the instrument that allowed BlackRock and Vanguard to eventually surpass it.

## The Custody Business

State Street operates one of the world's largest **custody and administration businesses** — $53.8 trillion in assets held in custody. Custody means State Street holds assets on behalf of others: pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, institutional investors. It is the back-office infrastructure of global institutional finance. The custody business gives State Street visibility into capital flows across the institutional financial system that its competitors lack.

## The ESG Lawsuit

State Street is the third defendant in Texas AG Ken Paxton's November 2024 antitrust lawsuit alongside BlackRock and Vanguard, alleging the three coordinated through ESG initiatives to reduce coal production. The Trump administration's FTC and DOJ filed a Statement of Interest supporting the lawsuit in May 2025.

State Street, like BlackRock, has retreated from its ESG positioning under political pressure. It withdrew from the Net Zero Asset Managers initiative.

## The Structural Numbers

State Street's combined Big Three statistics mirror those of its peers:
- Largest shareholder in **88% of S&P 500 companies** (as part of Big Three)
- Part of the network controlling **25% of voting shares in corporate America**
- Together with BlackRock and Vanguard: **51% of U.S. fund AUM**, **20%+ of U.S. market cap**

State Street's individual ownership stakes are smaller than BlackRock's or Vanguard's in most companies — typically 4-6% where BlackRock and Vanguard hold 7-9% — but as the third consistent large holder, its voting weight completes the concentration.

## The Fearless Girl

In March 2017, State Street placed the *Fearless Girl* statue facing the Wall Street Bull as part of a campaign to pressure S&P 500 companies to add women to their boards. The campaign was backed by its own voting power — State Street committed to vote against boards at companies failing to add women directors. The campaign worked: companies with State Street ownership increased female board representation.

The *Fearless Girl* campaign is a crystalline example of how Big Three institutions translate passive ownership into active governance influence without any democratic accountability for that influence. State Street's decision to use its voting power as leverage for a social policy outcome was made by State Street executives, accountable to State Street's shareholders — not to the companies' shareholders, employees, or the broader public whose corporate governance was being shaped.

## Vault Relevance

State Street completes the Big Three concentration structure. Without all three acting as large passive shareholders simultaneously, the common ownership phenomenon that reduces competition across industries and concentrates governance power would be less severe. State Street is the third leg.

It also invented the instrument — the ETF — through which the entire passive investing revolution, and thus the entire concentration problem, was built. The SPY is the financial equivalent of Oracle's CIA database: a foundational infrastructure product that enabled an empire neither its creators nor its regulators fully anticipated.
