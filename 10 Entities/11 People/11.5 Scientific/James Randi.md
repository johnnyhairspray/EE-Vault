# James Randi

Born August 7, 1928. Died October 20, 2020. Canadian-American stage magician and professional skeptic. Founded the James Randi Educational Foundation (JREF) and ran the **One Million Dollar Paranormal Challenge** from 1964 to 2015 — offering $1 million to anyone who could demonstrate supernatural abilities under controlled scientific conditions. Nobody ever collected.

## The Uri Geller Obsession

Randi devoted decades specifically to debunking [[Uri Geller]], arguing that everything Geller did — spoon bending, telepathy, remote viewing — could be replicated using standard stage magic and sleight of hand. Randi's core epistemological argument: the CIA scientists at SRI who tested Geller (physicists Targ and Puthoff) were not trained in detecting deception. A skilled magician would exploit their blind spots in ways a physicist wouldn't recognize.

Randi could bend spoons himself using conventional conjuring and demonstrated it publicly and repeatedly. His position: Geller is a skilled illusionist, not a psychic.

## The Analytical Problem

Randi and the CIA arrived at opposite conclusions about the same person. The CIA's declassified documents state Geller "demonstrated his paranormal perceptual ability in a convincing and unambiguous manner." Randi concluded he demonstrated nothing except the credulity of scientists.

Both cannot be fully correct. The vault notes this tension without resolving it — the question of whether Geller's abilities were real is less analytically significant than the documented fact that the CIA *believed they were real* and built a $20M intelligence program around that belief.

The intelligence community's investment in psi research had strategic logic independent of whether the abilities existed: if adversaries believed in psychic warfare and were spending accordingly, the U.S. needed to understand and counter it regardless of epistemological conclusions.

## The Million Dollar Challenge

The JREF challenge ran 1964-2015. No claimant ever passed the controlled testing protocol. Randi's position: the challenge demonstrated that under properly controlled conditions, claimed paranormal abilities disappear. Proponents' counter: the testing protocols were designed to make passing impossible.
