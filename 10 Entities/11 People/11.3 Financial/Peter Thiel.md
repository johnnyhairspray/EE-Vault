## 2025-2026 Current Developments

### Objection.ai — AI Tribunal for Journalism (April 2026)

The most significant current development. Thiel has funded **Objection.ai**, a new startup co-founded by **Aron D'Souza** — the Australian lawyer who organized Thiel's legal assault on Gawker Media (Bollea v. Gawker, 2016, resulting in Gawker's bankruptcy). Co-funded by **[[Balaji Srinivasan]]**.

**The mechanism:** Anyone can pay $2,000 to file an "objection" against a media story. This triggers an investigation by a team hired from the CIA, FBI, and British intelligence agencies. The targeted outlet and reporter are invited to respond. All evidence is fed to an AI model that renders a verdict — an "Honor Index" score reflecting the reporter's "integrity, accuracy, and track record." Complainants and targets are asked to agree to binding arbitration with unspecified consequences. The system also includes a feature called **"Fire Blanket"** that flags disputed claims in real time on X via platform APIs — casting doubt on stories even when no ruling has been issued.

**The weight of evidence:** The system privileges primary records (regulatory filings, official emails) over anonymous sources. Whistleblower claims rank near the bottom of the evidence hierarchy. Reporters who don't participate may receive an "indeterminable" result — which the system treats as shadow doubt even when the underlying reporting is accurate.

**Initial targets** as of April 2026:
- *New York Times* — for reporting on how David Sacks uses his White House AI/Crypto Czar position to benefit Silicon Valley connections
- *Wall Street Journal* — for its revelations about Trump's doodle in Jeffrey Epstein's birthday book (the case against WSJ was subsequently dismissed by a federal judge)
- British reporter Hannah Broughton — for aggregated reporting on Amazon workers required to continue working while a colleague lay dead
- Bernie Sanders and Candace Owens as social media targets

The pattern is clear: Objection.ai's first cases are deployed against reporting on Thiel's own network (Sacks/White House conflicts) and against the Epstein/Trump connection his network shares.

**Analytical note:** This is Gawker as template, industrialized, pointed at investigative journalism generally. Where Gawker cost $10 million in litigation over years, Objection.ai enables anyone with $2,000 to launch a harassment campaign cloaked in the language of accountability. The for-profit structure supports the story about purpose; financial sustainability is the least of the founders' concerns. The Coda Story analysis: *"It is classic Thiel: an attempt to hack the principles of accountability, and turn them against journalism."*

## Formation

Raised in Foster City, California in an Evangelical Christian household. B.A. Philosophy, Stanford University, 1989 — where he co-founded *The Stanford Review* and studied under **[[René Girard]]**, whose mimetic theory — that human desire is imitative, leading inevitably to rivalry and violence, resolved only through the scapegoat mechanism — became the philosophical substrate of Thiel's worldview. Girard's framework explains Thiel's apparent contradictions: he is not operating from ideology, he is operating from a theory of power in which the scapegoat mechanism can be deliberately engineered and exploited.

J.D., Stanford Law School, 1992. Met [[David Sacks]] at Stanford — the PayPal network begins here.

## The PayPal Mafia

Co-founded [[PayPal]] 1998 with [[Elon Musk]], [[David Sacks]], and others. Sold to eBay for $1.5 billion, 2002. The PayPal alumni network — the "PayPal Mafia" — became the seed capital and personnel network for Silicon Valley's next generation: Musk (Tesla, SpaceX, now DOGE), Sacks (AI/Crypto Czar, White House), [[Joe Lonsdale]] (Palantir co-founder), [[Ken Howery]] (Founders Fund, Ambassador to Denmark under Trump), [[Reid Hoffman]] (LinkedIn).

First outside investor in [[Facebook]], 2004 — $500,000 for 10.2% stake. Served on Facebook's board 2004-2022. On the board during the 2014 emotional contagion experiment and the 2016-2018 Cambridge Analytica scandal. Advised Zuckerberg against fact-checking political ads throughout. Left board May 2022.
