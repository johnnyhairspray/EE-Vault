# Marc Andreessen

Born July 9, 1971. Co-founder and general partner at Andreessen Horowitz (a16z). Co-creator of Mosaic (1993), the first widely-used web browser; co-founder of Netscape. One of the most influential venture capitalists in Silicon Valley.

## Political Alignment (2024–2026)

Andreessen shifted from a generally apolitical public posture to explicit MAGA alignment in 2024:

- Published "The Techno-Optimist Manifesto" (October 2023) — an accelerationist document explicitly framing deceleration of technology (including AI safety concerns) as evil
- Endorsed Trump in August 2024, alongside Ben Horowitz
- Andreessen and Horowitz committed $50M to the "Leading the Future" Super PAC in late 2025, focused on primarying politicians who advocate AI safety or regulatory frameworks for AI
- Appeared on Joe Rogan's podcast to advocate for Trump before the 2024 election
- Was a key lobbyist for JD Vance's VP selection — coordinating with Peter Thiel

The Vance connection is significant: Andreessen Horowitz backed [[10 Entities/11 People/11.1 Political/JD Vance]]'s Narya Capital fund alongside Thiel. Vance is now VP. a16z's portfolio companies benefit from an administration they helped install.

## The Saudi PIF Relationship

Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF), through its Sanabil Investments arm, disclosed portfolio stakes in approximately 40 venture and growth funds — including Andreessen Horowitz. a16z is simultaneously negotiating the $40 billion HUMAIN AI fund partnership with Saudi Arabia.

The implication: Saudi sovereign capital is an LP in the VC firm that backed the VP, while the same VC firm is negotiating a $40B deal with the Saudi sovereign wealth fund. The financial circularity is not incidental.

## Praxis

Andreessen is an investor in [[Praxis]] — the Network State project exploring Greenland as territory for a privately governed city-state. Fellow investors include Peter Thiel, Sam Altman, Joe Lonsdale, Balaji Srinivasan, and the Winklevoss twins. This places him inside the sovcorp ideological project, not merely adjacent to it.

## "Little Tech" Framing

Andreessen has developed a "Little Tech" political narrative — framing AI regulation as a mechanism for large incumbents to lock out smaller competitors. The argument: safety regulations disproportionately burden startups that can't afford compliance, while protecting the Googles and Microsofts that can.

The framing is strategically effective because it's partially true. It is also used to argue against any regulatory oversight of AI development — including safety requirements that would apply to frontier models regardless of company size.
